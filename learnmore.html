Overview of the Krebs Cycle

WHERE DOES KREBS CYCLE OCCUR? ▼

In Eukaryotic cells, the citric acid cycle occurs in the Mitochondrial matrix, which is the innermost compartment of the Mitochondrion. This is also where Pyruvate, a product of glycolysis, is converted into Acetyl-CoA before entering the cycle. In contrast, Prokaryotic cells, which lack membrane-bound organelles like Mitochondria, carry out both the conversion of Pyruvate to Acetyl-CoA and the Citric acid cycle in the Cytoplasm.

One important characteristic of the citric acid cycle is that it operates as a closed loop. This means that the final steps of the cycle regenerate the molecule needed for the first step, allowing the process to continue as long as reactants, such as Acetyl-CoA and necessary enzymes, are available. This continuous nature of the cycle ensures a steady production of high-energy molecules like NADH, FADH₂, and ATP, which are crucial for the next stage of cellular respiration—the electron transport chain.

In the first step of the cycle, Acetyl CoA combines with a four-carbon acceptor molecule, Oxaloacetate, to form a six-carbon molecule called Citrate. After a quick rearrangement, this six-carbon molecule releases two of its carbons as carbon dioxide molecules in a pair of similar reactions, producing a molecule of NADH each time. The enzymes that catalyze these reactions are key regulators of the Citric acid cycle, speeding it up or slowing it down based on the cell's energy needs.


The remaining four-carbon molecule undergoes a series of additional reactions, first making an ATP molecule—or, in some cells, a similar molecule called GTP then reducing the electron carrier FAD to FADH2, and finally generating another NADH. This set of reactions regenerates the starting molecule, Oxaloacetate, so the cycle can repeat.

Overall, one turn of the Citric acid cycle releases two carbon dioxide molecules and produces three NADH, one FADH2, and one ATP or GTP. The Citric acid cycle goes around twice for each molecule of Glucose that enters cellular respiration because there are two Pyruvates—and thus, two Acetyl CoAs—made per Glucose.

Citric Acid Cycle


Enzymes, which are proteins that catalyze chemical reactions in the body, are key players in the Krebs cycle and their role is essential for Oxidative hosphorylation to occur. They regulate all the steps of the cycle.

STEP 1

Formation of Citrate

Acetyl CoAjoins with a four-carbon molecule, oxaloacetate, releasing the CoA group and forming a six-carbon molecule called citrate

This reaction is catalyzed by the enzyme citrate synthase.

Citrate Synthase: Citrate synthase removes the acetyl group and then adds it to oxaloacetate compound to form citric acid.
STEP 2

Isomerization to Isocitrate

Citrate is converted into its isomer,isocitrate.

Citrate is converted to isocitrate by the enzyme aconitase.

Aconitase: Aconitase transfers an oxygen atom to make a more reactive molecule of isocitrate.
STEP 3

Oxidation of Isocitrate

Isocitrate is oxidized and releases a molecule of carbon dioxide, leaving behind a five-carbon molecule α-ketoglutarate. During this step, NAD+ is reduced to form NADH.

The enzyme catalyzing this step, isocitrate dehydrogenase, is important in regulating the speed of the citric acid cycle.

Isocitrate Dehydrogenase:Isocitrate dehydrogenase removes only one carbon atom to form carbon dioxide CO2 and also transfers the electrons to the NADH molecule.
STEP 4

Oxidation of α-Ketoglutarate

α-ketoglutarate is oxidized, reducing NAD+ to NADH and releasing a molecule of carbon dioxide. The remaining four-carbon molecule picks up Coenzyme A, forming the unstable compound succinyl CoA.

The enzyme catalyzing this step, α-ketoglutarate dehydrogenase, is also important in regulation of the citric acid cycle.

α-Ketoglutarate Dehydrogenase:Alpha-Ketoglutarate Dehydrogenase removes only one carbon atom to form carbon dioxide CO2, also transfers the electrons to NADH molecule and the molecule remaining part is connected to coenzyme A.
STEP 5

Substrate-Level Phosphorylation

The CoA of succinyl CoA is replaced by a phosphate group, which is then transferred to ADP to make ATP (or GDP to GTP in some cells). The four-carbon molecule produced is called succinate.

Succinyl-CoA is converted to succinate by the enzyme succinate synthetase.

Succinate Synthetase (Succinyl-CoA Synthetase):Because the bond between coenzyme A and succinate is unstable and needed to provide the energy for building ATP molecule, the succinyl-CoA synthetase enzyme is used to create the GTP molecule in the reaction (fifth step).

GTP is similar to ATP: both serve as energy sources, and the two can be readily interconverted.

STEP 6

Oxidation of Succinate

Succinate is oxidized, forming another four-carbon molecule called fumarate. In this reaction, two hydrogen atoms are transferred to FAD, producing FADH2.

Succinate is oxidized to fumarate by the enzyme succinate dehydrogenase.

Succinate Dehydrogenase: TSuccinate dehydrogenase plays a role in the electron transport chain by extracting the atoms of hydrogen from succinate compounds and transferring them to the FAD molecule which acts as carrier.

Why use FAD here? FAD is a better electron acceptor than NAD+ for this reaction because succinate is not a strong enough electron donor for NAD+.

STEP 7

Hydration of Fumarate

Water is added to fumarate, converting it into another four-carbon molecule called malate.

Fumarate is hydrated to form malate by the enzyme fumarase.

Fumarase (Fumarate Hydratase): Fumarase adds a molecule of water to the molecule to prepare it for the last step of citric acid cycle.
STEP 8

Regeneration of Oxaloacetate

Oxaloacetate the starting four-carbon compound—is regenerated by oxidation of malate. Another molecule of NAD+ is reduced to NADH in the process.

Malate is oxidized to oxaloacetate by the enzyme malate dehydrogenase.

Malate Dehydrogenase: Malate dehydrogenase is used in the final step for oxaloacetate recreation and electrons transferring to NADH by converting malate compound to oxaloacetate compound.

Oxaloacetate is then ready to react with another molecule of Acetyl-CoA, restarting the cycle.

PRODUCTS OF THE CITRIC ACID CYCLE ▼

The Krebs cycle is a series of chemical reactions that allow cells to use energy from carbohydrates. The cycle starts with the entry of glucose into the cell. This energy is used for different cellular processes such as synthesizing proteins and membranes and sustaining cellular functions. It produces carbon dioxide and water as waste products. In order to use the energy from glucose for these processes, it has to be converted into another type of energy—in the form of adenosine triphosphate (ATP). This is the main form of energy storage in the cell cycle regulation and provides the cells with the energy they need to carry out various processes. The energy produced by the conversion of glucose into ATP is called cellular respiration. The Krebs cycle is an essential part of the process of cellular respiration.

To fully understand the citric acid cycle, it is important to track the fate of carbon atoms and quantify the production of reduced electron carriers and ATP.
Per turn of the cycle (one acetyl-CoA molecule):

  • Carbon Flow: Two carbon atoms enter from acetyl-CoA, and two molecules of carbon dioxide (CO₂) are released.
  • Energy Carriers Produced:
  • 3 NADH
  • 1 FADH₂
  • 1 ATP (or GTP)

    • Per glucose molecule (two acetyl-CoA molecules):

  • Since each glucose molecule generates two acetyl-CoA molecules, the total yield must be doubled:
  • 6 NADH
  • 2 FADH₂
  • 2 ATP (or GTP)


It is important to note that while two CO₂ molecules are released in each turn of the cycle, these do not originate from the acetyl group of the newly entered acetyl-CoA. Instead, these carbons are first incorporated into the cycle's intermediates and will be released as CO₂ in later turns of the cycle. Over successive cycles, all carbon atoms from the original acetyl-CoA molecules will eventually be converted into carbon dioxide.

WHERE'S ALL THE ATP?

While the citric acid cycle directly generates only one ATP (or GTP) per turn, its primary role in ATP production is indirect.
The cycle produces high-energy electron carriers:

  • 3 NADH
  • 1 FADH₂

These molecules transfer electrons to the electron transport chain (ETC) in the final stage of cellular respiration. Through oxidative phosphorylation, the ETC utilizes these electrons to drive the production of a significant amount of ATP. Thus, although the citric acid cycle itself yields minimal ATP, it plays a crucial role in generating the electron carriers that power the majority of ATP synthesis in the cell.

Conclusion

The Krebs Cycle, also known as the Citric Acid Cycle, is a vital metabolic pathway that plays a central role in cellular respiration by extracting energy from Acetyl-CoA. Taking place in the mitochondrial matrix, this cycle systematically breaks down Acetyl-CoA through a series of chemical reactions, releasing carbon dioxide (CO₂) as a waste product while generating essential molecules like NADH and FADH₂, which drive ATP production in the Electron Transport Chain.

Additionally, the cycle directly produces a small amount of ATP, ensuring a continuous supply of energy for cellular processes. Beyond its function in energy metabolism, the Krebs Cycle serves as a biochemical intersection, linking carbohydrate, fat, and protein metabolism by providing key intermediates for various biosynthetic pathways. Its regulation is crucial for maintaining metabolic balance, ensuring cells produce ATP efficiently while preventing energy waste.

Since it is an essential pathway found in almost all aerobic organisms, disruptions in its function can lead to metabolic disorders and energy deficiencies, making it a major area of study in medical and biochemical research. Ultimately, the Krebs Cycle is more than just a series of chemical reactions—it is the foundation of energy production, sustaining life at the cellular level and powering every biological function necessary for survival.
